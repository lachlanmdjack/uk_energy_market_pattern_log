# Market Log — 1 July 2026

## Observations and Drivers

**Primary observation:**

> Monitoring D+1 power prices, prices have fallen from £123.94/MWh on the 30th to £104.35/MWh (as of 4pm), representing more than 15% fall in baseload price. An eye on the BMRS wind forecast shows a clear picture of why there has been an expectation of low prices as wind forecast generation reaches 20GW. N2EX also shows this, with low overnights (~£30/MWh) and negligible middays (7 straight hours of £0). Peaks then rapidly increase to £120/MWh, fueled by declining wind generation, increased gas generators to match peak demand and increased interconnector strain. 
> 
> It is evident that the above is short-term caused as the month ahead power price has only fallen 3%, indicating that any relative fall in day ahead pricing is short-lived and is not derived from an entire shift in the poewr forward curve. Additionally, day-ahead gas prices have increased 2%, so any gas-derived methods of power have come from spark instead of gas.
> 

**Second observation:**
> From the initial observation of the above, the impact on the spark spread must be non-negligible. 
>
> Spark Spread = Power price - (Gas price p/th / 0.0293071 / 100) / Efficiency
> 
> $SS_{start}$ = (104.35+19.59) - ((105.29-1.99) / 0.0293071 / 100) / 49.13% = 123.94 - 35.25 / 49.13% = £52.19
> 
> $SS_{end}$ = (104.35) - ((105.29) / 0.0293071 / 100) / 49.13% = 104.35 - 35.93 / 49.13% = £31.22
>
> Given carbon costs have only moved 1%, £55.58/tCO2 ~ £21.90/MWh. The story from this is gas plants were going to generate £30/MWh tomorrow, but due to the increase in wind generation will produce £10/MWh, at baseload. Ultimately, not running for midday (as has been confirmed is £0 for multiple hours) will negate much of this fall, running at minimum through this period to capture higher load factors during the peak. As stated, the peak is expected to be ~£120/MWh according to N2EX, so at +£20 to the baseload the clean spark will be ~£30, a drop from the possible £50 previously. 
> 
> Ultimately this may cause a steep ramp up in SSP as a considerable number of plants have to go from non-operational to full production for peak, likely leading to outturn prices out-'performing' day ahead. The 2nd could be a fantastic opportunity for BESS and storage hydro to charge midday for £0, and discharge at peak for arbitrage opportunity. If wind doesn't perform to the level it's expected to, this could trigger higher priced scenarios too.
